History of cell theory:

In 1665, Robert Hooke used a three-lens compound microscope and when examining tree cork found tiny hollow compartments which he named cells.

Around the same time, Anton van Leeuwenhoek made a single lens microscope as strong as the compound microscope. He became one of the first people to describe living cells when he observed single celled organisms in a pond of water.

As the microscope improved, it became easier to see greater magnification, making scientists wonder where cells come from.

German scientist Matthias Schleiden used a compound microscope and created the theory that plants were made of cells. When he worked with another scientist named Theodor Schwann, Schwann noticed similar structures in plant and animal cells. This made him publish the theory that all living things are made of cells which set the groundwork for later theories. But he thought cells form spontaneously while German scientist Rudolf Virchow proved him wrong saying all cells come from preexisting cells.

Cell Theory:

1. All living things are made up of cells

2. Cells are the most basic unit of life

3. All cells come from pre-existing cells

Structures of CELLS:

Found in Plant Cells Found in Both Found in Animal Cells

1-Chloroplast- Organelles that carry out photosynthesis. Outer and inner membrane, sacs are called thylakoids containing chlorophyll, a light absorbing molecule that gives it its green color and plays a key role in photosynthesis. This also has its own DNA and ribosomes and the same theory with mitochondria is also valid for this. Plants have both Mitochondria and Chloroplast.

2-Cell Wall-Surrounds the cell membrane and forms a rigid layer that gives protection, support, and shape to the cell. Made of cellulose and polysaccharide in plants, chitin in fungi, and peptidoglycan in bacteria.

1-Cytoplasm- Fluid that fills space between nucleus and cell membrane. Fluid portion is called cytosol and is mostly water.

2-Cytoskeleton (Already talked about)

3-Vesicles- Used to deliver materials from place to place. Short lived and recycled. Comes from ER and uses transport to Golgi from ER.

4-Nucleus-Storage for genetic information or DNA. DNA contains genes that are instructions for making protein. Keeps materials that could damage DNA out of nucleus. Double membrane surrounding called nuclear envelope filled with pores allowing molecules to pass between the nucleus and the cytoplasm.

5-Nucleolus-Dense region where tiny organelles that make proteins are made.

6-Endoplasmic Reticulum (Rough and Smooth)- Network of thin folded membranes. Maze of enclosed space where the inside is called the lumen. This makes proteins and lipids and all it is folded allows it to fit inside a cell. Surface with Ribosomes are called rough ER while the ones that do not and make lipids instead are called the smooth ER.

7-Ribosome- Tiny organelles that link amino acids together to form proteins. At the site of protein synthesis and active participants of the process. Ribosomes are made of proteins and RNA. Pass through the nuclear pores into cytoplasm.

8-Centrosome- Small region of cytoplasm that produces microtubules.

9-Cell Membrane-Semi-Permeable layer that keeps things from coming in or out of a cell

10-Golgi Apparatus-Closely layered stacks of membrane enclosed spaces that process, sort and deliver protein.

11-Mitochondria-Supply energy for the cell. Bean shaped with two membranes using chemical reactions to convert the food you eat into energy. Have their own ribosomes and DNA. Suggested that they were originally free-living prokaryotes that were taken in by larger cells to help both survive.

12-Vacuole-A fluid filled sac used for the storage of materials a cell needs. Most animal cells have small ones while large central ones are in plant ones. When a plant wilts, the leaves shrivel because there is not enough water in the vacuole to support the structure.

1-Centriole-Found in the centrosome, these are cylinder shaped organelles made of short microtubules arranged in a circle and they help during cell division to pull apart the DNA. They can be found in some algae but not in plants. They also help organize microtubules to form cilia and flagella.

2-Lysosome-Membrane bound organelles that contain enzymes. They defend cells from invading bacteria and viruses and break down damaged or worn-out cell parts. Some plant cells have them but fewer than in animal cells. They are formed in the rough ER inactive but when packaged and activated they need a membrane to keep them from destroying necessary things.

Passive Transport:

Passive transport can be through Osmosis or Diffusion. Diffusion is the movement of fluid or gas where places from high concentration to low concentration while Osmosis is the movement of water from high concentration to low concentration of water. There are 3 types of solutions isotonic, hypertonic, or hypotonic. Isotonic solutions occur when there is the same amount of a solute both in and outside of the cell. Hypotonic solutions are when there is less of a solute outside the cell, so water moves inside. The result is a bursting cell. Hypertonic is the opposite of hypotonic, in which water moves outside of the cell, causing it to shrivel. Molecules that have a hard time use facilitated diffusion using transport proteins to move certain molecules.

Active Transport:

There is active transport where it mostly uses chemical energy. There are two types, Endocytosis and Exocytosis. The first is the entering of chemicals into a cell while the second is the release of chemicals out of a cell. Part of endocytosis is phagocytosis and pinocytosis. Phagocytosis is engulfing solid material while pinocytosis is liquid.

Cellular Respiration:

C6H12O6 + 6O2 → 6CO2 + 6H20 + energy

Steps:

Glycolysis -> In cytoplasm, anaerobic, separates glucose into 2 pyruvates (net yield: 2 pyruvate, 2 ATP and 2 NADH)

Pyruvate oxidation (intermediate step) -> Pyruvate transported into mitochondria, oxidized into acetyl CoA

Krebs cycle (citric acid cycle) -> In the mitochondrial matrix, aerobic, inputs acetyl CoA and outputs 2 ATP, 6 NADH, 2 FADH2, with CO2 as a byproduct.

Electron Transport Chain & Chemiosmosis-> In inner mitochondrial membrane, aerobic, Electrons are transferred from NADH and FADH2 into protein complexes in electron carriers. These are used to pump out protons through ATP synthase. This makes ATP. Water is a product

NET: 30-38 ATP

Fermentation: (when there is no oxygen available)

Lactic Acid Fermentation: Occurs in muscle cells, makes 2 ATP and lactic acid as a byproduct, buildup of lactic acid causes your muscles to burn

Alcoholic fermentation: Occurs in some bacteria and yeast, makes 2 ATP and ethanol(alcohol) a byproduct.

Photosynthesis:

6CO2 + 6H20 -(light) → C6H12O6 + 6O2

Light reaction: Light splits water molecules into hydrogen and oxygen. Oxygen is released.

Dark reaction (Calvin Cycle): No light needed, Carbon dioxide is broken down, ATP is used to make glucose from the broken down C02 and hydrogens

Remember, the dark reaction occurs ALL throughout the day, not just in the absence of light. Both reactions are needed to produce glucose.

The energy of ATP (adenosine triphosphate) is released when the link between the second and third phosphate groups is severed. The connection between the two is unstable and breaking it releases energy. When it breaks, it becomes adenosine diphosphate (ADP). ADP can become ATP again if the third phosphate group is added back. This means that the ADP is always recycled, and ATP is a temporary state.

Interphase – PMAT - Cytokinesis

Interphase: G1, S, and G2 phases. In S phase, the DNA is duplicated.

Meiosis:

PMAT twice

Prophase 1: Everything in Prophase + crossing over and chromosomes pairing up

Metaphase 1: Everything in Metaphase + random lineup of chromosomes

Anaphase 1: Everything in Anaphase

Telophase 1: Everything in Telophase

Meiosis 2:

Divides sister chromatids instead of homologous chromosomes, happens in both daughter cells from Meiosis 1 and creates 2 daughter cells each. All four of these daughter cells contain half the number of chromosomes as the original parent.

Cancer

• Mutation in the DNA when duplicating it can be harmful, though most of the mutations are minor and most mutated cells are killed by the body’s immune system. Some do not become cancer cells. Cancer cells duplicate unrestrictedly and therefore faster than regular cells which means that they are dangerous.

• Benign tumors = mostly good, does not spread and invade organs

• Malignant tumors = Bad, spreads and invades organs and stuff

• Carcinogens- anything that causes cancer; Ex. Viruses (15% of all human cancers), UV radiation (leading cause of skin cancer), chemicals (such as ones in food; cooking foods at elevated temperatures)

• Cancer is dangerous because it makes the body give nutrients to it without providing anything back to said body.

Mutations that lead to cancer usually occur in genes that control the cell cycle. These include tumor-suppressor genes and proto-oncogenes.

1. Tumor-suppressor genes normally prevent cells with damaged DNA from dividing. Mutations in these genes prevent them from functioning normally. As a result, cells with damaged DNA are allowed to divide. Proto-oncogenes normally help control cell division. Mutations in these genes turn them into oncogenes. Oncogenes promote the division of cells with damaged DNA.

Pathways of blood:

Blood has two different paths that meet at the heart. These are called pulmonary and systemic circulation. All blood travels on both paths.

Pulmonary Circulation- Occurs between the heart and lungs and main function is to carry oxygen poor blood to the lung and pick up the O2 to carry back to the heart.

Systemic Circulation- Between the heart and the rest of the body to carry oxygen to all the cells and transport poor blood back to the heart. In extreme conditions, the systems can serve vital functions in making sure the vital organs stay at a constant temperature.

Factors affecting blood flow:

Blood pressure: Increased blood pressure = increased blood flow = smaller blood vessels Decreased blood pressure = decreased blood flow = larger blood vessels

Blood pressure is the force the blood pushes against the wall and a healthy pressure for a resting young adult is 120 over 70 millimeters of mercury. 120 is known as the systolic pressure which is the amount of pressure on the artery walls when the left ventricle contracts. 70 is known as diastolic pressure which is the pressure in the artery when the left ventricle relaxes. These changes are based on the elastic and unblocked arteries and the strength of the heart. The less elastic and more blockage the harder the blood flow and harder the heart pumps raising blood pressure. Stress and strong emotions can also make it rise but it drops with rest. Permanent high pressure is called hypertension which can lead to a heart attack or stroke. A stroke is where the blood flow to the brain is interrupted, and a heart attack is where the arteries are damaged or blocked.

Lifestyle affects it since smoking, lack of exercise, excessive weight, and long-term stress, and a diet low in fruits and veggies and high in saturated fats increase the risk of circulatory diseases. Diseases affect the heart. To reduce the risk physicians, urge people to not smoke, remain a healthy weight, and exercise regularly.

Blood volume: Usually varies little, Increased volume = increased flow, decreased volume = decreased flow

Resistance: Increased thickness/viscosity = increased resistance = decreased blood flow Decreased thickness/viscosity = decreased resistance = increased blood flow.

Exercise: Increased exercise = widened blood vessels =increased blood flow

After the egg cell is fertilized, it becomes a zygote, which undergoes mitotic division as it travels to the uterus. Once it becomes a blastocyst (a hollow ball of cells) and implants itself on the uterine lining. Once differentiation begins, the zygote becomes an embryo.

Maternal and fetal blood do not mix.

Chorion membrane: nourishes embryo and has villi that extends into uterine lining

38 weeks (about 8 and a half months) of development from zygote to fully formed fetus.

This is split up into 3 trimesters (~3 months each).

2nd Trimester: Heartbeat heard via stethoscope, Brain grows fast, mother can feal movement, fingers and toes fully develop, swallows' amniotic fluid, and makes urine

Babies born prematurely at the start of the third have a hard time surviving while babies born toward the middle to end often survive and thrive. At the last month, the lungs are strengthened and the fetus sucks in and pushes out of the amniotic fluid. (Water breaks, child breathing)

DNA replication:

• Helicase unzips the DNA by breaking the fragile hydrogen bonds, creating a replication fork.

• DNA primase tells the DNA polymerase where to start and it starts connecting complementary nucleotides together. It goes in a 5’(phosphate) to 3’(carbon), so there will be a lagging strand as shown below.

• Ligase glues together the Okazaki fragments and two new identical strands of DNA are created. This is known as semi-conservative because each new strand has half of the old strand of DNA in it.

Transcription:

• A section of DNA is unzipped, and RNA polymerase identifies where to start.

• RNA polymerase, going in the 5’ to 3’ direction, and pairs the bases, replacing thymine with uracil.

• Once the entire gene is transcribed, the RNA strand detaches from the DNA and the DNA is zipped back together.

Translation:

1. mRNA enters the ribosomes, and the exposed codon (a triplet of bases) attracts a complementary tRNA.

2. The ribosome makes a peptide bond between the amino acids and one tRNA goes away.

3. This repeats until it reaches a stop codon.

PLANT REPRODUCTION:

A key feature of all plant life cycles is alternation of generations.

Sporophyte: A multicellular diploid (2n) stage. “Spore plant,” meaning a plant that will make spores.

Gametophyte: A multicellular haploid (n) stage. “Gamete plant,” meaning a plant that will make gametes that will eventually fuse to form a zygote.

Gametes (sex cells = egg and sperm) are produced from haploid gametophyte during mitosis.

Meiosis produces spores that will develop into haploid multicellular organisms.

Alternation of Generations:

It happens in every multicellular organism

Basically, describes the process between haploid and diploid cells.

NONVASCULAR REPRODUCTION:

Gametophyte dominated

Water carries sperm from a male gametophyte to a female gametophyte to facilitate fertilization

After, when the sporophyte forms from mitosis, it remains attached to the gametophyte, relying on it for water and nutrients

Once spores are made, they are dispersed via water (or air if the humidity is high enough) and germinate to yield more gametophytes, and the cycle continues

VASCULAR REPRODUCTION:

Seedless:

Reproduce most similarly to nonvascular plants

Sporophyte dominated

Spores made by the sporophyte grow on the underside of a fern’s leaf and get released to find a moist patch of ground to germinate on

These spores create a tiny gametophyte from mitoses and has male and female reproductive organs on the underside of the leaf

Sperm on one side finds an egg on the other side and fuse to form the zygote, which develops into the sporophyte.

Pollen grains carry sperm from male cones at the bottom of the upward to fertilize eggs in ovules in the female cones towards the top of the tree

After fertilization, a “naked” seed grows, which will later ripen into a mature plant

Seed provides key nutrients and protection for the growing plant

Pollen gets dispersed by wind and does not need water to reproduce

Angiosperms:

Sexual reproduction in angiosperms occur when the pollen from an anther is transferred to the stigma. The pollen moves down the style of the pistil into the ovary. It then fertilizes an ovule, which then develops into a seed.

Sporophyte dominated – Every part of the plant is the sporophyte except for the pollen and ovum in the flower

Flower is the key reproductive structure

Does not need wind or water, pollination strategies are used to attract other organisms to spread pollen to other ovules

After fertilization, the ovule becomes seed, and the ovule becomes fruit (anything the ovary turns into even nuts)

POLLINATION:

Pollination is when water, wind, or an organism carries pollen from one plant to another to make fertilization possible

Fertilization leads to seed development, which leads to fruit development (and thus seed dispersal) in flowering plants

Angiosperms have unique adaptations to allow them to use a variety of pollination strategies in their specific environments

Self-Pollination: Plant pollinates itself. Produces smaller and lesser amounts of seeds, no genetic diversity

Cross-Pollination: Requires pollinators (or wind or water). Produces more seeds and healthier offspring. Genetically diverse. In many flowers, the pistils and stamens reach maturity at different times. This prevents the plant from pollinating itself, forcing itself to get pollinated by another plant

Flowering plants: Relies on insects or animals to pollinate. Produces large colorful flowers.

Non-flowering plants: Relies on wind or water to pollinate

Seed dispersal is the movement of seeds away from the parent plant

Plants utilize a variety of different methods to do this

Advantages of using seed and fruit dispersal: Makes the offspring grow farther from the parent plant to reduce competition between them both.

PLANT ORGAN FUNCTIONS:

It is thought that plants have evolved from aquatic algae. The earliest land plants had spores only.

Then vascular plants appeared next. They evolved leaf, stem, root, and vascular structures to become more competitive.

Fruit: Advanced seed dispersal

Flower: Showy display and scent to attract pollination. Attaches to the stem of the plant. Are brightly colored to attract organisms to pollinate the plant.

Cone: Protection for seeds. Allows seeds to be protected by tough tissues until they are mature enough to be dispersed.

Seed: Packaged genetic material for generating a plant somewhere else; delayed growth. Over spores, seeds have the advantages of delayed growth of the new baby plant, greater protection from the environment, and the ability to be dispersed over great distances.

Root: Improved mineral and water uptake. Keeps the plants anchored and absorbs water and nutrients. The center of root is vascular cylinder, which is made of xylem and phloem tissues. Surrounded by ground tissue, covered by dermal tissue. These cells have tiny protections called root hairs, making their way between soil particles, increasing the surface area. Root caps are a small cone of cells that protect the growing part of the root. Behind the root cap is where most of the root’s growth occurs. Groups of cells that are the source of new cells form tissue called meristem. The types of roots are fibrous root systems and taproot systems.

Stem: Improved support and water transport. Transports nutrients, stores nutrients, and creates new growth using special cells. Pattern of plant growth depends on the location of meristems in the plant. Stems have two growths.

Leaf: Greater surface area for photosynthesis. Collects sunlight in chloroplasts, photosynthesis, and contains stomata for gas exchange.

PLANT CELL TYPES:

Plant tissues are made up of three basic cell types.

Parenchyma Cells: Most common type. Least specialized plant cells but can divide for the entirety of their lives. Are important to healing and regeneration. Stores starch, oils, and water for plants. It has thin walls and large water filled vacuoles. Photosynthesis occurs in green chloroplasts within parenchyma cells in leaves. Chloroplasts and colorless plastids within roots and stems store starch.

Collenchyma Cells: Has cell walls that range from thick to thin, providing support for while still allowing plant to grow. Most common in younger tissues of leaves and shoots. Are flexible. It doesn’t contain lignin in cell wall, so they can stretch and change size.

Sclerenchyma Cells: Strongest plant cell. It has a second cell wall that’s hardened by lignin. Can’t grow within plants. Most die when they reach maturity. Cytoplasm and organelles disintegrate when they die, but the rigid cell walls are left behind as skeletal support. Found in stems and leaf veins.

TISSUE SYSTEMS AND FUNCTIONS:

Ground tissue system: Support, storage, and photosynthesis. Used for metabolism. Any tissue that is not dermal or vascular. Types of this tissue include Parenchyma (traditional plant cells), Collenchyma (support, like cellulose), and Sclerenchyma (wooded and durable, like what makes up an apple core). Parenchyma tissue is the most common of the ground tissues. Makes up inside of a plant.

Dermal tissue system: Protection, water loss prevention. Includes epidermis (mostly) and periderm (like bark). Made up of live parenchyma cells. On leaves and some stems, epidermal cells may secrete a wax-coated substance that becomes the cuticle. Dermal tissue made of dead parenchyma cells makes up the outer bark of woody plants.

Vascular tissue system: Support, water, and nutrient transport. Xylem transports water from the roots to the shoot. Phloem moves minerals from roots to the shoots and moves sugars made during photosynthesis from leaves in from the shoot to other parts of the plant.

Ground, vascular, and dermal tissue all occur in roots, stems, and leaves.

Mendel’s Laws of Heredity:

Gregor Mendel is known as the “Father of genetics.” He was an Australian monk who used pea plants to learn about genetics.

His research showed that traits are inherited as discrete units. He laid the groundwork for genetics and his data revealed patterns of inheritance.

Law of Dominance – Dominant alleles will always mask recessive alleles

Law of Segregation – Each gamete receives one allele

Law of Independent Assortment – Genes independently separate from each other and are sorted independently. (In dihybrid crosses)

Autosomal Recessive:

Most common pattern, disease is rare and skips generations, males and females are equally likely to inherit the disease

Only shows with two recessive alleles (ex. rr)

Carriers are heterozygous (ex. Rr)

25% for two carriers to have a child with the trait

Autosomal Dominant:

The disease is common in the family, never skips a generation, and males and females are equally likely to inherit the disease. Very ineffective though as the person who has the disease might pass away before passing on the disease.

Less common due to being eliminated by natural selection

People can’t really be a carrier because if they had a dominant trait then they’ll be affected.

Trait/disease is expressed if the person is homozygous dominant or heterozygous (ex. RR, Rr)

Autosomal Chromosome Disorders:

Autosomal chromosome disorders are caused by a mutation happening to an autosomal chromosome and thus it ends up affecting multiple genes. There are many types of mutations that can cause an autosomal chromosome disorder.

Nondisjunction is one of the most common types of causes for autosomal chromosome disorders

Nondisjunction is the failure of homologous chromosomes to separate correctly during meiosis, resulting in an abnormal number of chromosomes in the resulting gametes

Happens during anaphase

Ex. 3 chromosomes instead of 2; 1 chromosome instead of 2

23 pairs of chromosomes (46 chromosomes) in a normal person

Sex Chromosome Disorders:

Sex chromosome disorders are caused by a mutation happening specifically to a sex chromosome. Typically, the mutation is nondisjunction, and results in an organism having an abnormal number of sex chromosomes. (ex. XXY and XXXY)

A person with Turner’s syndrome only inherits one X chromosome.

They are biologically female rather than male because they only have one X chromosome. Usually applies to girls missing one X chromosome. They don’t contain a Y chromosome, meaning that they miss out on all the traits that make up a male.

Not inherited because it happens randomly based on how a girl’s genes are first formed.

People with Klinefelter’s syndrome inherit extra X chromosomes, and can have genotypes like XXY and XXXY.

Sex(X)-Linked Recessive:

Disease is rare, often skips generations, males are often more affected than females, affected fathers never pass the disease to their sons
